*Discourse on the Method* (1637) by René Descartes is the book that launched **modern Western philosophy.** It contains the most famous sentence in the history of philosophy — *"I think, therefore I am"* (Cogito, ergo sum) — and a revolutionary method for discovering truth that still shapes how we think today.

## What Descartes Was Trying to Do

Descartes was frustrated. He had received the best education Europe could offer, yet he found himself filled with **more doubt than certainty.** Philosophy seemed to be nothing but endless disagreement. Even science was built on assumptions that no one had properly verified.

His goal: start from scratch and build knowledge on a foundation of **absolute certainty.** If he could find even one thing that was impossible to doubt, he could build everything else from there.

## The Method: 4 Rules for Clear Thinking

Descartes proposed four rules for discovering truth:

### Rule 1: Accept Nothing as True Unless Clearly Known to Be So

> "Never to accept anything for true which I did not clearly know to be such."

Don't assume something is true just because authority says so, because tradition says so, or because it *feels* true. Only accept what you can verify through clear, rational analysis.

### Rule 2: Divide Problems into Smaller Parts

Break every complex problem into its smallest components. Analyze each part separately before trying to understand the whole.

### Rule 3: Build from Simple to Complex

Start with the simplest, most certain truths and build upward toward more complex conclusions. Each step must follow logically from the previous one.

### Rule 4: Review Everything Thoroughly

Check your work. Make sure no step has been skipped and no assumption has slipped in unnoticed.

**Modern relevance:** These four rules are essentially the **scientific method** and the foundation of **critical thinking.** Every scientist, engineer, and logical thinker uses some version of Descartes' method.

## "I Think, Therefore I Am"

Descartes applied his method of radical doubt to everything:

- Can I doubt that the physical world exists? **Yes** — it might be a dream.
- Can I doubt that my body exists? **Yes** — my senses could be deceiving me.
- Can I doubt that mathematics is true? **Yes** — some evil genius might be tricking me.
- Can I doubt that *I am thinking*? **No** — the very act of doubting proves I exist as a thinking being.

> "I think, therefore I am."

This one certainty became his foundation. From it, he rebuilt his entire philosophy: the existence of the self, the existence of God (as guarantor of truth), and the reliability of clear and distinct ideas.

## Why It Still Matters

1. **Critical thinking:** Descartes' method teaches you to question assumptions, break down problems, and think systematically — skills that are essential in every profession.

2. **Intellectual humility:** The willingness to say "I might be wrong about everything" is the starting point for genuine knowledge.

3. **The mind-body question:** Descartes' conclusion that mind and body are separate substances launched a debate that continues in neuroscience, AI, and philosophy of mind today.

4. **Scientific methodology:** His insistence on systematic doubt and clear reasoning helped create the modern scientific approach.
