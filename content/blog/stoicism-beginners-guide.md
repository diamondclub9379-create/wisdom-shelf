Stoicism is having a massive revival. Everyone from Silicon Valley CEOs to professional athletes to therapists is talking about it. But what *is* Stoicism, really? And how do you actually practice it?

This guide cuts through the noise and takes you straight to the source — the original Stoic texts that are now in the public domain and free to read.

## What Is Stoicism?

Stoicism is a school of philosophy founded in Athens around 300 BC. Its core teaching is simple:

**Focus on what you can control. Accept what you can't. And develop the wisdom to know the difference.**

Sound familiar? The Serenity Prayer uses almost the same words. That's because Stoicism has quietly shaped Western culture for over two thousand years.

The three most important Stoic thinkers whose works survive are:

1. **Epictetus** (55–135 AD) — A former slave who became one of the greatest philosophers. His student's notes became [The Enchiridion](/ebooks/the-enchiridion/).
2. **Marcus Aurelius** (121–180 AD) — Roman Emperor whose private journal became [Meditations](/ebooks/meditations/).
3. **Seneca** (4 BC–65 AD) — Roman statesman and advisor to Emperor Nero.

## The 4 Core Stoic Principles

### 1. The Dichotomy of Control

> "Some things are within our power, while others are not." — Epictetus, [The Enchiridion](/ebooks/the-enchiridion/)

This is the foundation of everything. Epictetus divides the world into:

**Within your control:** Your thoughts, your judgments, your actions, your responses.

**Not within your control:** Other people's behavior, the weather, the economy, your reputation, what happened in the past, what will happen in the future.

Most suffering comes from trying to control things in the second category. The Stoic solution: redirect all your energy toward the first.

### 2. Virtue Is the Only Good

The Stoics identified four cardinal virtues:
- **Wisdom** — Knowing what is truly good, bad, and indifferent
- **Courage** — Acting rightly despite fear
- **Justice** — Treating others fairly and with compassion
- **Temperance** — Self-control and moderation

Everything else — wealth, health, pleasure, fame — the Stoics classified as "preferred indifferents." Nice to have, but not necessary for a good life.

### 3. We Suffer from Our Judgments, Not from Events

> "It is not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things." — Epictetus

When someone cuts you off in traffic, the event itself is neutral — a car changed lanes. The anger comes from your *judgment*: "That person disrespected me!" Remove the judgment, and the suffering disappears.

This insight is the foundation of modern Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), which was directly inspired by Stoic philosophy.

### 4. We Are All Connected

The Stoics believed in a universal reason (*logos*) that connects all human beings. We are not isolated individuals but parts of a larger whole. This creates a duty to serve others and contribute to the common good.

Marcus Aurelius, despite being Emperor, constantly reminded himself of his obligations to serve Rome and treat all people — from senators to slaves — with dignity.

## 5 Practical Stoic Exercises

### Exercise 1: Morning Premeditation

Each morning, spend 5 minutes mentally preparing for the day:

- What difficulties might arise?
- What annoying people might you encounter?
- How will you respond with wisdom and patience?

Marcus Aurelius practiced this daily:

> "When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: the people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant..." — [Meditations](/ebooks/meditations/)

This isn't pessimism — it's preparation. You won't be caught off guard by problems you've already anticipated.

### Exercise 2: The View from Above

When you're stressed or overwhelmed, mentally zoom out. Imagine looking down at yourself from above. Then zoom out further — see your city, your country, the entire Earth. See the vast sweep of history — billions of people who lived and died before you.

This exercise puts your problems in perspective. Whatever you're worrying about is temporary and tiny in the cosmic scale.

### Exercise 3: Evening Review

Before bed, review your day:
- Where did I act virtuously?
- Where did I fail?
- What could I do better tomorrow?

This is not about self-criticism but about honest self-assessment. Marcus Aurelius practiced this nightly, and *Meditations* is largely the product of this exercise.

### Exercise 4: Voluntary Discomfort

Periodically practice mild discomfort on purpose:
- Take a cold shower
- Skip a meal
- Sleep on the floor
- Walk instead of driving

The purpose is to prove to yourself that comfort is not necessary for happiness. When you know you can handle discomfort, you stop fearing it — and fear of discomfort drives most of our bad decisions.

### Exercise 5: The "Is This Within My Control?" Check

When you feel anxious or frustrated, pause and ask: **"Is this within my control?"**

- If **yes**: Take action
- If **no**: Accept it and redirect your energy

This simple check, practiced consistently, can dramatically reduce anxiety and stress.

## Stoicism vs. Modern Self-Help

| Modern Self-Help | Stoicism |
|---|---|
| "Think positive!" | "Think *clearly*" |
| "You can have anything you want" | "Want only what is within your power" |
| "Eliminate negative emotions" | "Understand and manage your judgments" |
| "Visualize success" | "Prepare for difficulty" |
| "Love yourself" | "Improve yourself through virtue" |

Stoicism is not about suppressing emotions or being cold. It's about **understanding which emotions are based on accurate judgments and which are based on false ones** — then correcting the false ones.

## Start Reading the Stoics

The two essential Stoic texts are both in the public domain and free to download:

1. **[The Enchiridion by Epictetus](/ebooks/the-enchiridion/)** — Start here. It's short (about 20 pages), practical, and direct. Think of it as a Stoic handbook.

2. **[Meditations by Marcus Aurelius](/ebooks/meditations/)** — Read this next. It's deeper, more reflective, and endlessly quotable. Best read slowly, a few passages per day.

For a different angle on similar ideas, also check out:
- [Self-Reliance by Emerson](/ebooks/self-reliance/) — American individualism with Stoic echoes
- [As a Man Thinketh by James Allen](/ebooks/as-a-man-thinketh/) — The power of directed thought
