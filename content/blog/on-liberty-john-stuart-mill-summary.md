*On Liberty* (1859) by John Stuart Mill is the most influential defense of **individual freedom** in Western philosophy. In an era of increasing social media censorship, cancel culture, and debates about the limits of free speech, Mill's arguments are more relevant than they've been in decades.

## The Core Principle

Mill proposes one simple rule for when society can interfere with individual liberty:

> "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others."

This is known as the **Harm Principle.** Your freedom to act is unlimited — up to the point where your actions harm someone else. Beyond that point, society has the right to intervene. Before that point, it does not.

This means:
- You can think whatever you want
- You can say whatever you want
- You can live however you want
- **As long as you're not harming others**

## The Defense of Free Speech

Mill's most powerful chapter argues that **suppressing any opinion is always wrong,** even if 99% of people disagree with it. He gives four reasons:

### 1. The Suppressed Opinion Might Be True

No person or group is infallible. History is full of "obviously wrong" opinions that turned out to be true: the Earth revolving around the sun, germ theory, and democracy itself were all once considered dangerous ideas.

### 2. Even False Opinions Contain Partial Truth

Almost every opinion, no matter how wrong overall, contains some grain of truth. Suppressing it means losing that grain. Free debate lets us extract what's valuable and discard what's not.

### 3. Unchallenged Truth Becomes Dead Dogma

Even if an opinion is completely true, if it's never challenged, people hold it as a prejudice rather than a reasoned conviction. **You don't truly understand your own beliefs until you've defended them against the strongest objections.**

### 4. Without Debate, Truth Loses Its Power

A truth that is merely inherited — never questioned, never defended — eventually loses its motivating force. It becomes a dead formula rather than a living conviction.

## Individuality and Self-Development

Mill argues that **individuality is inherently valuable** — both for the person and for society. Conformity produces mediocrity. Diversity of thought and lifestyle drives progress.

> "He who lets the world, or his own portion of it, choose his plan of life for him, has no need of any other faculty than the ape-like one of imitation."

This echoes Emerson's [Self-Reliance](/ebooks/self-reliance/), written 18 years earlier. Both thinkers argue that nonconformity is not rebellion — it's the highest expression of human potential.

## The Tyranny of the Majority

Mill identifies a danger beyond government tyranny: **social tyranny** — the pressure of majority opinion that forces individuals to conform.

> "Society can and does execute its own mandates... it practices a social tyranny more formidable than many kinds of political oppression."

**Modern relevance:** Social media mobs, cancel culture, and online shaming are precisely the kind of "social tyranny" Mill warned about. The pressure to conform to popular opinion — enforced not by laws but by social punishment — is as dangerous to liberty as government censorship.
