The **revision technique** is one of Neville Goddard's most practical and powerful methods. While most people know Neville for his "State Akin to Sleep" (SATS) technique for manifesting future desires, revision works in the opposite direction — it **rewrites the past** to change its effects on your present and future.

## What Is Revision?

Revision is the practice of **mentally replaying a past event, but changing it to match what you wish had happened.** You don't just think about the revised version — you *relive* it with all the feeling and sensory detail of a real memory.

Neville introduced this technique primarily in *[Seedtime and Harvest](/ebooks/seedtime-and-harvest/)*, where he explains:

> "Revision is of all the most wonderful. Revision is — seeing an event or conversation which took place and you wanted to take place a different way — you revise it, you replay it in your imagination the way you wished it would have gone."

## Why Does Revision Work?

According to Neville's philosophy, **the past is not fixed.** Your memory of the past is not a recording — it's a living construct in your consciousness. When you revise a memory with sufficient feeling, you are not "pretending" the past was different. You are **actually changing the causal chain** that leads from past events to future outcomes.

Think of it this way:
- A bad job interview → creates a feeling of failure → attracts more failure
- **Revised** job interview (you imagine it going perfectly) → creates a feeling of success → attracts more success

Even if the original event "happened," its power to influence your future comes from **how you remember it**. Change the memory, and you change its influence.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Choose an Event to Revise

Pick something that happened today (or recently) that didn't go the way you wanted:
- A difficult conversation
- A rejected proposal
- An embarrassing moment
- A conflict with someone
- A missed opportunity

### Step 2: Get Into a Relaxed State

Lie down or sit comfortably. Close your eyes. Take several deep breaths. Let your body relax completely. The drowsy state between waking and sleeping is ideal, just like with SATS.

### Step 3: Replay the Original Event

Briefly recall how the event actually went. Don't dwell on it — just orient yourself in the scene.

### Step 4: Now Revise It

Replay the same event, but this time **change it to match your ideal outcome:**

- If the conversation was tense, reimagine it as warm and productive
- If you were rejected, reimagine being accepted enthusiastically
- If you said something embarrassing, reimagine saying something confident and well-received
- If there was conflict, reimagine harmony and agreement

### Step 5: Make It Vivid and Feeling-Based

This is the crucial step. Don't just *think about* the revised version — **relive it:**

- **See** the other person's face smiling, nodding
- **Hear** their words of agreement, praise, or acceptance
- **Feel** the handshake, the warmth, the satisfaction
- **Experience** the emotions: relief, joy, pride, gratitude

The more real it feels, the more effective it is.

### Step 6: Accept It as the True Memory

This is where many people stumble. You must let go of the "actual" version and accept the revised version as what really happened. Don't hold both versions — **replace** the original with the revision.

### Step 7: Repeat if Needed

For significant events, you may need to revise them multiple times over several days until the revised version feels more natural than the original.

## Daily Revision Practice

Neville recommended revising your entire day each night before sleep:

1. Lie in bed at the end of the day
2. Review your day **in reverse** — start with the most recent event and work backwards
3. When you encounter an event that was unpleasant or imperfect, **stop and revise it**
4. Reimagine it as you wish it had gone
5. Continue backwards through the day, revising as needed
6. Fall asleep after completing the review

This nightly practice has several benefits:
- It prevents negative momentum from carrying into tomorrow
- It trains your mind to focus on desired outcomes rather than problems
- It builds the skill of visualization and feeling
- Over time, you'll notice that fewer events *need* revision — your days naturally improve

## Real Examples

**Example 1: A difficult boss meeting**
- *Original:* Your boss criticized your report in front of the team
- *Revised:* Your boss says, "Great work on this report. The analysis was exactly what we needed."
- *Feel:* The pride, the team nodding in agreement, the warmth

**Example 2: A rejected date invitation**
- *Original:* "Sorry, I'm busy that weekend."
- *Revised:* "I'd love to! What time works for you?"
- *Feel:* The excitement, the smile, the planning

**Example 3: A missed sale**
- *Original:* The client said they'd "think about it" and never responded
- *Revised:* The client says, "Let's do it. Where do I sign?"
- *Feel:* The handshake, the signed contract, the commission

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Am I lying to myself?**
No. You're choosing which version of events to carry forward in your consciousness. The "true" version has no more inherent power than the revised version — its power comes from your emotional investment in it.

**Q: Does revision actually change the past?**
Neville would say yes — consciousness is the only reality, so changing consciousness *is* changing reality. Practically speaking, what changes is the past's *influence on your future*.

**Q: Can I revise events from years ago?**
Yes. Childhood wounds, past traumas, old regrets — all can be revised. For deep-seated events, you may need to revise them many times.

**Q: What if I can't remember the details?**
That's fine. Focus on the *feeling* of the event and revise that. You don't need perfect visual recall.
