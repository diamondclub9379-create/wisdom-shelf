In 1918, Theron Q. Dumont published *The Power of Concentration* — a book arguing that **the ability to focus your mind on one thing is the single most important skill you can develop.** Over a century later, in our age of smartphones, social media, and constant notifications, his message has never been more relevant.

Dumont (a pen name for William Walker Atkinson) doesn't just theorize about concentration — he provides **specific, practical exercises** to build your mental focus like a muscle.

## The Core Argument

Dumont's central thesis:

> "Concentration is the ability to hold your attention on one subject to the exclusion of everything else."

He argues that every successful person in history — every inventor, leader, artist, and entrepreneur — achieved their results primarily through superior concentration. Talent matters less than focus. Resources matter less than attention. The person who can concentrate most intensely on their goal will always outperform the scattered genius.

## Why Concentration Matters More Than Ever

When Dumont wrote this book in 1918, the biggest distractions were noisy neighbors and daydreaming. Today we face:

- Smartphones checking (average: 96 times per day)
- Social media feeds designed to be addictive
- Email, Slack, and constant messaging
- 24/7 news cycles
- Multitasking culture that rewards busyness over depth

The ability to concentrate deeply for extended periods is now a **competitive superpower**. Cal Newport calls it "deep work." Dumont simply called it concentration.

## Key Principles from the Book

### 1. Concentration Can Be Trained

Your ability to focus is not fixed at birth. It's a skill that can be developed through practice, just like physical strength. Dumont provides progressive exercises, starting simple and increasing in difficulty.

### 2. Physical Relaxation Enables Mental Focus

Tension in your body scatters your mental energy. Before concentrating, relax your muscles completely. A calm body produces a calm, focused mind.

### 3. Interest Fuels Concentration

You concentrate naturally on things that interest you. The trick is to *generate interest* in whatever you need to focus on. Find the fascinating angle. Connect it to your goals.

### 4. Willpower Is Limited — Use It Wisely

Don't waste concentration on trivial decisions. Develop habits and routines for daily tasks so you can reserve your full concentration for important work.

### 5. Thoughts Are Real Forces

Like other New Thought authors of his era, Dumont argues that concentrated thought has a direct effect on reality. A thought held with intense focus becomes a creative force that attracts the circumstances needed for its realization.

## 5 Concentration Exercises from the Book

### Exercise 1: The Glass of Water (5 minutes)

1. Fill a glass of water and hold it at arm's length
2. Focus your entire attention on keeping the water perfectly still
3. No ripples, no trembling
4. Hold for 5 minutes

This seems simple but is surprisingly difficult. It trains the connection between mental focus and physical control.

### Exercise 2: The Finger Focus (5 minutes)

1. Sit in a chair and raise your right arm to shoulder height
2. Turn your head and fix your gaze on your fingers
3. Hold your arm perfectly still while focusing only on your fingertips
4. Start with 1 minute and build to 5

This exercise builds sustained attention and physical stillness simultaneously.

### Exercise 3: The Object Focus (10 minutes)

1. Place a small object (a coin, a pen) on a table in front of you
2. Focus your entire attention on the object
3. Study every detail — color, texture, shape, reflections
4. When your mind wanders (it will), gently bring it back
5. No judgment, just return

This is essentially a mindfulness meditation, described a century before mindfulness became mainstream.

### Exercise 4: The Mental Image (10 minutes)

1. Close your eyes and visualize a simple object — an apple, a candle
2. Hold the image steady in your mind as long as possible
3. When it fades or morphs, bring it back
4. Try to see finer and finer details — the texture of the apple skin, the flicker of the candle

This trains *internal* concentration — the ability to hold a mental image, which is essential for all visualization and manifestation techniques.

### Exercise 5: The Thought Chain (15 minutes)

1. Choose a topic — any topic
2. Think about it systematically, following a logical chain of thought
3. Do not allow your mind to wander to any unrelated topic
4. If you catch yourself drifting, note where you drifted and return to the chain
5. Continue for 15 minutes

This exercise builds the capacity for sustained, directed thinking — the kind of thinking that produces breakthroughs.

## A Daily Practice

Dumont recommends practicing concentration exercises for at least 15 minutes daily. Start with the simpler physical exercises and progress to the mental ones. Within a few weeks, you should notice:

- Improved ability to focus during work
- Less susceptibility to distractions
- Better memory (attention is the foundation of memory)
- Increased productivity with less effort
- A calmer, more centered mental state
